# Socratic techniques — finance-grill

How to handle vague answers, surface real risk tolerance, draw out goals the user hasn't articulated, and gently challenge contradictions. Read this before starting the interview.

---

## 1. Probing vague answers

**Vague answers are the default.** Most people say "save for the future" or "I want to be financially comfortable." Don't accept these — they're not actionable.

### Pattern: ladder of specificity

When you get vague, ask one specificity question at a time. Don't pile them all up.

**User:** "I want to save for the future."

**Bad:** "Got it." *(records nothing useful)*

**Bad:** "What time horizon, what amount, what's it for, how flexible, what's the criticality?" *(overwhelming)*

**Good:**
- "Future how far out — 5 years, 20 years, retirement?"
- *[after answer]* "And roughly how much, in today's rupees? Rough is fine."
- *[after answer]* "What's the future state you're saving for? A house, kids' education, just security?"

### Pattern: anchor with examples

If a user genuinely doesn't have a number in mind, give a range to react against — but not a single number, which becomes anchoring.

**User:** "I have no idea what foreign undergrad costs."

**Good:** "Rough sticker shock numbers — US private undergrad is around ₹1.2–1.5 Cr in today's rupees, UK is more like ₹70L–1 Cr, Singapore/Australia somewhere in between, IIT/IIM-equivalent here is ₹15–30L. Which range feels closest to what you'd want for your child?"

### Pattern: convert percentages to rupees

People say percentages because they sound concrete but feel safer. They're worse for planning.

**User:** "I save about 30% of my income."

**Good:** "That's a great rate. In rupees per month, what does 30% work out to roughly?"

## 2. Surfacing real risk tolerance

Self-reports are unreliable. People say "moderate" because it feels balanced, not because they've examined it. Use these techniques.

### Behavioural history is the gold standard

What did they actually do in past downturns?

- "March 2020 — when markets dropped 30%+ in a few weeks, what did you actually do? Sell? Hold? Buy more?"
- "Did you change your SIPs? Stop them? Increase?"
- "How did you feel — panicked, calm, opportunistic?"

The story matters more than the verdict. "I held but I checked my portfolio four times a day for two months" is a different signal from "I held and didn't really notice for a quarter."

### Scenarios with concrete numbers

Vague hypotheticals get vague answers. Use concrete rupee numbers tied to their actual portfolio size.

If they have ₹50L invested:

- "Imagine your portfolio is at ₹35L tomorrow morning — a 30% drawdown, fully unrealised. What's your honest first move?"
- "What's the rupee number where you would actually panic-sell? Maybe it's ₹40L, maybe ₹25L. There's no wrong answer — knowing the number is the useful thing."

### Choose-between forced trades

Forces revealed preference rather than stated preference.

- "If you had to pick — guaranteed 8% per year vs expected 12% per year with the chance of 25% loss in any single year. Which do you actually want?"
- "10-year lock-in giving you 8% tax-free vs fully liquid giving you 7% taxable. Which?"

### Watch for the "I'll be fine, I'm long-term" trap

Many users claim long-term mindset but check portfolios daily and react to news. Surface it gently:

- "You've said you're long-term oriented. How often do you actually look at your portfolio?"
- "When markets had a bad week last month, did you look more often than usual?"

If there's a gap between stated mindset and behaviour, that's data. Note it. The agent should size equity allocation based on **revealed** tolerance, not claimed tolerance.

## 3. Drawing out unstated goals

Many users have a "save for the future" frame and haven't articulated specific goals. Use discovery prompts.

### Time-projection prompts

- "Imagine your life 10 years from now and it has gone really well financially. What's different from today?"
- "Imagine 30 years from now. What does that life look like? What are you doing daily? Where do you live?"

These surface lifestyle goals that don't fit neat categories.

### Constraint-removal prompts

- "If you woke up tomorrow with ₹5 crore in your account, what would you do with it?"
- "If money were no object, what would you stop doing? What would you start doing?"

Reveals underlying values and unstated aspirations.

### Regret prompts

- "What's your biggest financial regret so far?"
- "What's the one thing you wish you'd started 5 years ago?"

Useful because future plans often mirror correction of past regrets.

### Threat prompts

- "Imagine a doctor calls and says you have to stop working in 5 years. What changes about your plan?"
- "Imagine you lose your job tomorrow — how long can you go on existing buffers?"

Surfaces protection gaps and clarifies emergency-fund / insurance priorities.

**Don't ask all of these.** Pick 2 that fit the user's energy.

---

## 4. Categorising goals by criticality

Help the user distinguish:

- **Must-have** — failure causes serious harm. (Retirement at *some* level. Children's primary education. Emergency fund.)
- **Should-have** — important but flexible. (Retirement at desired lifestyle level. Children's foreign undergrad if domestic is also acceptable.)
- **Nice-to-have** — would be great but life works without it. (Sabbatical. Vacation home. Early retirement vs standard.)

Pushback question if everything is "must-have": "If you had to drop one, which goes first?" The answer reveals true priority.

## 5. Surfacing contradictions kindly

The most valuable thing this interview does. Frame as observation, not judgment.

### Common patterns to scan for

| Tension | How to surface |
|---|---|
| Goals require returns higher than risk tolerance allows | "To hit ₹5 Cr in 15 years from your current corpus + surplus, you'd need ~13% CAGR. That's an equity-heavy portfolio with real drawdown risk — you mentioned a 30% drop would make you panic-sell. Help me reconcile those." |
| Aggressive investing without emergency fund | "You want to put fresh money into equity, but I notice there's no separate emergency fund. If you needed ₹3L for a hospitalisation tomorrow, where would it come from? Just to think this through together." |
| Long-term mindset + daily portfolio checking | "You described yourself as long-term oriented, and you also said you check the portfolio daily. Both of those are real — but they're in tension. Which do you want the agent to design around?" |
| Wants ELSS for tax + needs liquidity | "ELSS has a 3-year lock-in. You also said you might need to access investments quickly. ELSS doesn't fit that need — want to think about whether tax savings are worth the lock-in here?" |
| Insurance gap invalidates plan | "Just flagging — you don't currently have term life and have two dependents. Even the best investment plan has a single-point-of-failure here. The agent will probably surface this in the first review. Up to you whether to address it before we proceed." |

### Tone

- Not "you're wrong" — "I'm noticing a tension"
- Not "you should" — "which side do you want to revise"
- Not lecturing — observing and asking
- Brief — don't get into a debate during the interview

---

## 6. When to stop probing

Don't drag the conversation. If the user has answered with reasonable specificity and you have what you need:

- Acknowledge briefly ("Got it.")
- Move to the next batch
- Don't ask follow-ups for the sake of it

If the user resists detail or seems fatigued:

- Mark the field UNKNOWN and move on
- Add to the open-items list
- Reassure: "We can revisit this — better to keep momentum."

The interview should feel structured but not interrogative. 20–40 minutes is the target. If you're at 60+ minutes, you're probing too hard.

---

## 7. What never to do during the interview

- **Don't recommend products.** Not even hints. ("ELSS would be great for you" — no.) The interview captures state. Recommendations are for downstream skills.
- **Don't moralise on existing holdings.** Even ULIPs, endowment policies, expensive Regular plan MFs — just record. The portfolio-review skill will surface optimisations later.
- **Don't anchor on specific scheme names** when asking about goals. ("How much do you want in PPF?" is wrong. "How much retirement corpus do you want?" is right. Allocation comes later.)
- **Don't shame.** No raised eyebrows about a low emergency fund or a high credit card balance. Just record and let the contradictions pass do the work.
- **Don't fabricate.** If the user genuinely doesn't know their tax bracket, do a rough estimate together with them — don't guess and write it as fact.
